Content Distribution:
Beyond the Default Stack

Eight channels serious practitioners actually use — none of which require a Gumroad account.

Working Document
Entries: 8  ·  Passive income claims: 0  ·  Substacks mentioned: 0  ·  Gumroad links: 0

Most content distribution advice optimizes for low friction at the cost of low yield. The channels below assume you have something genuinely worth distributing — and that your goal is audience quality, not audience volume.

Distribution Channels
01
Licensed Syndication
B2B

Pitch your work to wire services (AP, Reuters Connect, Tribune Content Agency) or regional newspaper syndicates for placement across their subscriber networks. A single piece can run in dozens of outlets simultaneously under your byline, with a licensing fee paid to you — not the other way around. The model is old and underused by digital-native writers who don't know it exists.

Best for: Analysis, commentary, columns with consistent voice  ·  Upside: One piece, many placements, recurring licensing revenue

02
Trade Press Op-Ed Placement
B2B

Every vertical industry has two or three trade publications that serve as the canonical reading for practitioners — Pensions & Investments for asset managers, Risk.net for compliance desks, Global Telecom Business for the connectivity industry. These outlets actively seek expert outside contributors and give you an audience that is pre-qualified, senior, and often exactly who you'd want to reach. Zero follower count required; credibility does the work.

Best for: B2B expertise, professional credibility-building  ·  Upside: Reaches decision-makers, not scrollers

03
The Conference Speaking Circuit
Active

Speaking at industry events transforms content from something people read into something people experience — and remember. Your talk is the content. The recording becomes an artifact. The Q&A builds relationships. It compounds: one keynote generates video clips, pull quotes, invitations to panels, and warm inbound that a hundred newsletters couldn't manufacture. The circuit is less competitive than it looks because most practitioners never try.

Best for: Established expertise, thought leadership, client development  ·  Upside: Relationships, inbound, paid speaker fees at senior levels

04
Ghost-Authored Executive Content
B2B Revenue

Senior executives at PE firms, consultancies, and think tanks have ideas and platforms but no time to write. A skilled journalist can ghost-author LinkedIn essays, white papers, and op-eds that publish under the executive's name. The work gets wide distribution through their existing network; you get paid (rates run $500–$5,000 per piece at the senior end) and accumulate off-record relationships worth more than any byline. This is a legitimate, well-established market hiding in plain sight.

Best for: Journalists with strong B2B sector knowledge  ·  Upside: High per-unit pay, relationship depth, durable repeat work

05
LinkedIn Native Publishing
Active

Genuinely underrated and widely misunderstood. LinkedIn's algorithm heavily surfaces long-form native articles and substantive posts to professional audiences who share them within their vertical networks — without you needing a follower base. This is not the platform for thought leadership clichés or humble-brags. The content that travels is specific, credentialed analysis: "Here is something I know that most people in this industry don't." The B2B reach-per-effort ratio is difficult to match anywhere else.

Best for: B2B audiences, compliance, finance, policy, risk  ·  Upside: Organic discovery, professional network compounding, zero cost

06
Strategic Podcast Guesting
Active

Not building a podcast — appearing on the right ones. The 500,000th true-crime podcast does not need you. But a tightly scoped industry podcast with 8,000 highly specific listeners (intelligence community professionals, PE associates, OSINT practitioners) is a more valuable platform than a general publication with ten times the readership. Identify the three podcasts your ideal client actually listens to on their commute and pitch them. One excellent 45-minute appearance creates clips, transcripts, and introductions that work for months.

Best for: Any niche with an active podcast ecosystem  ·  Upside: Trust transfer from host, evergreen archive, targeted reach

07
Closed Intelligence Distributions
B2B Revenue

The highest-value content is often the most restricted. A formatted PDF brief, memo, or situation report distributed directly to a curated subscriber list via email — no platform, no algorithm, no SEO — commands premium pricing precisely because of its exclusivity and directness. Stratfor built its entire business on this model. The Economist Intelligence Unit. The Briefing Room. Access is the product; the writing is the mechanism. Distribution by invitation or paid subscription only, with no public-facing archive.

Best for: Political risk, compliance, market intelligence, sector analysis  ·  Upside: Highest per-reader revenue, premium positioning, no platform dependency

08
High-Trust Community Seeding
Active

Certain Slack workspaces, Discord servers, and private forums operate as the real information layer of an industry. OSINT Twitter, compliance Slack groups, political risk Discord communities — the practitioners in these spaces share material that actually moves their work forward, and a single piece landing in the right channel can travel further and faster than anything algorithmic. This is not spam; it's finding the rooms where your work is genuinely useful and becoming a trusted contributor before you distribute anything.

Best for: Niche technical or professional communities  ·  Upside: Word-of-mouth through high-credibility networks, no follower requirement

The common thread in every channel above is that distribution follows credibility, not the reverse. You are not building an audience by posting consistently and hoping the algorithm rewards you. You are placing specific, expert work where specific, expert people already gather — and letting the quality do the acquisition.

For anyone operating in B2B intelligence, compliance, or political risk: channels 02, 04, and 07 alone represent a full content strategy. The others compound them.
